Projects

During my studies and employment I developed multiple tools and frameworks that tackle different issues within the information security domain. The majority of those tools are open-source and can be found on my Github page GitHub or here. Here is a list of some of those tools and frameworks:

  • Maat (April 2019 - Present): a framework that mines VirusTotal scan reports to extract various information about the correctness, completeness, and consistency of scanners.
  • GoldRusher (January 2015 - January 2018): GoldRusher is a dynamic analysis tool primarily meant to aid reverse engineers with analyzing (malicious) binaries.
  • Droidutan (December 2016 - Present): Droidutan (Android + Orangutan) is a simple Python API built to test Android apps. The API is built on top of AndroidViewClient.
  • Repackman (January 2018 - August 2018): Repackman is a tool to automatically repackage Android apps with arbitrary (malicious) payloads for research purposes.
  • Oedipus (April 2015 - December 2016): A Python framework that uses machine learning algorithms to implement the metadata recovery attack against obfuscated programs.

About

Born and raised in Cairo, I developed an interest in information security during my teenage years, and attempted to play the role of a Hollywood hacker using crude tools such as Sub7. To be honest, despite managing to social engineer my way into getting some passwords and sensitive information, I probably infected my computer with a heap of malware rather than successfully broke into my friends' devices! I majored in computer science at the American University in Cairo (AUC) and attempted to gather as much academic information about the security domain as possible. First and foremost, that was not anywhere near enough. Secondly, apart from a couple of small companies, every single security-related position I interviewed for had that part "Well, son, you need experience to work within the security domain. This experience you get by working in the security domain!" What?! Well, maybe I can go back to school and study more about the domain, right? So, I went for a master's degree in information security at University College London (UCL) between 2011 and 2012.

During my master's at UCL, I decided upon further pursuing the academic research aspect of information security by going for a doctoral degree. Unfortunately, I could not secure funding for any of the doctoral programs that accepted me in the UK. So, I moved back to Cairo to work as an information security researcher for a mere of one year before finally moving to Munich, Germany for a second master's degree at the TU Munich, and a doctoral degree I am currently working on.

My primary research focus is on utilizing machine learning techniques and algorithms within the domains of malware analysis/detection, Android security, and penetration testing. I am also infinitely interested in topics such as privacy, usable security, secure software development, and software integrity protection.

I pride myself on being an engineer more than an academic researcher. That is, no matter how absurd an idea seems to be, I seek to implement it! Writing and publishing papers is by all means secondary to developing practical solutions. That makes my dream job something that overlaps software development and information security at a firm that attempts to develop solutions to real-world problems no matter how big or small they are.
